1.5 Key Industry Trends (2025–2030)

  • AI PC Integration: 54% of enterprises integrating AI chips into procurement cycles; AI-PC penetration expected to reach 93.9% by 2028 (Lenovo forecasts 80% by 2027).
  • Memory Crunch: DRAM and NAND flash component costs for mid-range PCs rose an estimated 40–70% during 2025, driven by AI data center demand outpacing foundry output.
  • Windows 10 Sunset (Oct 2025): End-of-support triggered the largest enterprise hardware refresh cycle in a decade, boosting commercial PC sales across all major vendors.
  • Geopolitical Reshaping: US–China chip export restrictions continue to redraw global supply chains. TSMC committed $65 billion to Arizona fabs to reduce geopolitical concentration risk.
  • Green Computing Surge: 46% demand increase for energy-efficient hardware as data center power consumption becomes a regulatory and ESG priority.
  • Edge & IoT Expansion: Compact AI-enabled devices for industrial IoT, healthcare, and smart cities represent the fastest-growing non-consumer segment.

NVIDIA's Historic Rise: In 2024, NVIDIA claimed the #1 global semiconductor revenue position for the first time — overtaking Samsung and Intel. The company's extraordinary GPU demand for AI workloads represents a seismic value shift in the semiconductor industry: from general-purpose CPUs to AI-optimized accelerators. This single trend is reshaping capital allocation across the entire global computing supply chain.

2.5 Technology & Industry Trends

3.2 Major Greek Shipyards

⚓ Skaramagas Shipyards

Founded 1939. Acquired by shipowner George Prokopiou in March 2023 and commercially relaunched February 2024. In its first year, completed work on 37 vessels including Suezmax tankers and mega-yachts. Dry Dock No. 1 is the only Greek facility capable of accommodating a 274.2-metre Suezmax hull. Ambitions to become a Mediterranean leader in complex repairs and LNG carrier servicing.

🛡️ Salamis Shipyards

Greece's most internationally significant defense builder. Via a collaboration with France's Naval Group (from Oct 2022), Salamis fabricates hull blocks for the FDI Belharra frigates with a remarkable 80.4% pre-equipping completeness — among the highest recorded globally. A six-year Follow-on-Support agreement signed Nov 2024 covers both Hellenic Navy and French FDI fleets in Eastern Mediterranean and Red Sea.

🏗️ ONEX Shipyards (Neorion/Syros + Elefsina)

Acquired by NY-based ONEX group in 2019. Together the two yards have repaired 612 vessels since acquisition. Combined turnover leapt from a historic maximum of €20–21M to over €120M in 2024 — nearly a 600% increase — through capital injection, workforce expansion, and modern dry-dock upgrades. ONEX now positions Syros + Elefsina as a unified cluster capable of new construction.

4.5 Key Trends Reshaping Publishing

  • BookTok Effect: TikTok's #BookTok community directly influenced approximately 59 million US print book sales in 2024 — creating overnight bestsellers and reviving backlist titles years after initial publication.
  • Self-Publishing Boom: Platforms like Amazon KDP, Smashwords, and Draft2Digital have democratized authorship. Self-published titles now represent a significant and growing share of e-book sales globally.
  • AI in Publishing: AI tools are being adopted across manuscript editing, cover design, marketing copy, and audio narration. Publishers are debating, legislating, and experimenting with generative AI for content creation and metadata optimization.
  • Subscription Models: Kindle Unlimited, Scribd, and Audible continue growing. 63% of audiobooks are now consumed via subscription — fundamentally changing revenue-per-book economics for authors and publishers.
  • Print Resilience: Despite decades of predictions of print's demise, physical books maintain ~75% of consumer trade market share. Special editions, collector's formats, and aesthetically designed books thrive among younger demographics.
  • Big Five Consolidation: Penguin Random House, HarperCollins, Simon & Schuster, Hachette, and Macmillan continue to dominate trade publishing, though facing increasing competition from mid-size independents and direct-to-reader models.

5.4 Reading Culture & Habits

  • Genre Preferences: Greek readers favor crime/thriller fiction (led by Petros Markaris, Greece's internationally celebrated crime author), historical fiction, and contemporary translated fiction. Self-help and personal development are the fastest-growing nonfiction categories.
  • Translation Market: A significant proportion — estimated 35–45% — of books sold in Greece are translations, predominantly from English, French, and Scandinavian languages. Scandinavian crime fiction has a particularly strong Greek readership.
  • Youth Reading Decline & Recovery: Reading rates among 15–25 year-olds dropped sharply during austerity years (2010–2017) but showed recovery signals post-2020. Social media BookTok influence is beginning to appear in Greek youth reading trends.
  • Thessaloniki International Book Fair: One of the most important book events in Southeastern Europe, the annual Thessaloniki fair is a key industry gathering, cultural event, and international rights market for Greek publishers.
  • Greek Literature Internationally: Greek authors receive growing international attention. Works by authors such as Amanda Michalopoulou, Christos Ikonomou, and Ersi Sotiropoulos are translated and distributed globally. Greek mythology-inspired fiction remains perennially popular worldwide.
  • Publishing Consolidation: Like elsewhere in Europe, Greek publishing has seen gradual consolidation, with larger groups acquiring smaller imprints. Fragmentation remains high relative to Western European peers, with hundreds of micro-publishers co-existing alongside the major houses.

Greece's Unique Publishing Context: Greece publishes an impressive number of titles relative to its population of ~10.5 million — approximately 10,000+ new Greek-language titles annually. The country has a deep literary tradition, high university graduation rates, and a culture of public intellectual discourse that supports a vigorous independent press. The main structural challenge is market fragmentation: too many publishers competing for a relatively small domestic readership, which suppresses per-title profitability and limits investment in author development and international rights exploitation.
